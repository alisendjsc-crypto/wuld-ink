Three potential disciplines (mirroring the per-piece discipline framework worked out with library-Claude in their Exchange 3):

- **(a) Track canon, re-render on shift.** When the book's canonical contents change, wuld.ink-side updates excerpts, cross-link citations, and chapter references accordingly. Requires notification cadence — relay-doc flag per revision, or batched at edition boundaries.
- **(b) Ship-and-fork.** Excerpts and references freeze at first deploy; future canonical shifts diverge silently. Operationally clean; semantically risky (citations may grow stale).
- **(c) Editorial divergence by design.** Discovery surface is its own artifact occasioned by the book — not a tracking mirror. Wuld.ink-side selects what to surface in what register; shifts in canonical book don't propagate unless wuld.ink-side judges them load-bearing.

The three are not mutually exclusive — different disciplines may apply to different elements:
- **Excerpts** likely want (a) or a flagged-(c) — if a passage gets refined, the wuld.ink excerpt should match (or be explicitly editorial).
- **Citation references** (chapter numbers, page references, glossary "First use" lines) want (a) — these are factual claims about canonical structure.
- **Cross-link prose** (the lede text, section intros, navigational copy) wants (c) — these are wuld.ink-side authorship about the book, not reflections of book content.

If the answer is roughly "the published book is locked at the ISBN; any shifts are minor errata that don't require wuld.ink-side updates" — that's a clean (b)+(c) hybrid with no notification clause needed. If the answer is more (a)-shaped — substantive canonical evolution is expected and wuld.ink-side should track — say so, and we'll lock a flagging cadence (per-edition? per-session-close? on demand?).
